## Ars Technica

**Subject:** Every textbook gets Marconi's transatlantic signal wrong — computational study explains why

Dear Science Desk,

New computational research suggests the standard textbook explanation for Marconi's 1901 transatlantic radio transmission — skywave ionospheric reflection — is likely wrong. The signal was more probably carried by a ground wave mode (TM₀) in the Earth-ionosphere waveguide.

The finding comes from a larger study reconstructing Tesla's 1899 Colorado Springs apparatus, which reveals a dual-mode coupling mechanism not previously described in the literature. Tesla's spark gap modulation produced ELF subharmonics that land almost exactly on Schumann resonance frequencies (400/51 = 7.84 Hz vs. the 7.83 Hz fundamental). The alignment is striking.

The study is rigorous but accessible — 20 computational experiments with full open-source code and 47 plots.

## Popular Mechanics

**Subject:** Tesla's 1899 machine worked better than anyone thought — here's the computational proof

Dear Editor,

A new computational study reconstructs Nikola Tesla's Colorado Springs magnifying transmitter and finds it was far more effective at coupling energy into the Earth-ionosphere system than previously recognized.

The study identifies a mechanism where Tesla's apparatus simultaneously excited two types of electromagnetic waves — surface waves along the ground and resonant cavity waves between Earth and ionosphere. At extremely low frequencies, these waves occupy the same physical space and exchange energy at coastlines. This explains how Tesla could detect signals at continental distances from landlocked Colorado Springs.

The catch: Tesla was right about signaling but wrong about power transmission. His "99.97% efficiency" claims don't survive the physics.
